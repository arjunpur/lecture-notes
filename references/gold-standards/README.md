## Why this reference is strong

The MIT matrix-calculus notes are a useful gold standard because they are:

1. **Narrative, not transcript-shaped** — they read like a coherent set of notes or a short book, not a slide dump.
2. **Strongly structured** — title page, introduction, table of contents, clear numbered sections, and logical chapter flow.
3. **Motivated before technical** — they explain why a topic matters before diving into derivations.
4. **Concept-first** — key ideas are framed through remarks, recalls, notation tables, and definitions instead of only equations.
5. **Worked through step by step** — derivations and examples are explained in prose, not just stated.
6. **Connected to applications** — machine learning, optimization, adjoints, ODEs, and automatic differentiation are tied back to the core calculus ideas.
7. **Visually supportive** — figures and layout choices reinforce the mathematical point rather than decorating the page.
8. **Dense but navigable** — the notes are compact, but easy to scan and revisit because the hierarchy is strong.
9. **Consistent in notation and tone** — symbols, terminology, and exposition stay stable across the document.
10. **Extensible** — the document accumulates into a durable reference that remains useful after the lecture is over.

## How Bodhi should use this

When generating lecture notes, Bodhi should aim for the same qualitative properties without copying the exact voice or layout:

- produce a coherent pedagogical arc
- prioritize conceptual scaffolding and motivation
- integrate examples and derivations into the narrative
- use strong hierarchy and signposting
- connect abstract math to practical use cases
- create notes worth keeping as a long-term reference, not just a one-off class artifact
